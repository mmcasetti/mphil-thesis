Let $(A,B)$ be bimatrix game, and let $X=\Delta^1$ and
$Y=\Delta^2$ be the mixed strategy
simplices of player 1 and 2, respectively, that is,
\begin{equation}
\arraycolsep.2em
\label{mixed-strat-simplices}
\begin{array}{rcll}
X&=&\{\, x\in\reals^m&\mid x\geq\0,\ \1\T x = 1 \},\\
Y&=&\{\, y\in\reals^n&\mid y\geq\0,\ \1\T y = 1 \}.
\end{array}
\end{equation}
A {\em labeling} of the sets $X$ and $Y$ assigns to each
$x\in X$ and $y\in Y$ a set of {\em labels}, which is a
subset of $[m+n]$, as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the $m$ pure strategies of player 1 are denoted as $i\in [m]$;
\item the $n$ pure strategies of player 2 are denoted as $m+j$ with $j\in [n]$;
\item each mixed strategy $x\in X$ of player 1 has
    \begin{itemize}
    \item label $i$ for each $i\in [m]$ such that $x_i = 0$,
    \item label $m + j$ for each $j\in [n]$ such that the $j$-th pure strategy
    of player 2 is a best response to $x$;
    \end{itemize}
\item each mixed strategy $y\in Y$ of player 2 has
    \begin{itemize}
    \item label $m + j$ for each $j\in [n]$ such that $y_j = 0$,
    \item label $i$ for each $i\in [m]$ such that the $i$-th pure strategy
    of player 1 is a best response to $y$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
This labeling can be used to characterize the Nash equilibria of the game:
A pair $(x,y)$ is called {\em completely labeled} if each
possible label in $[m+n]$ is a label of $x$ or of~$y$.

\begin{theorem}{\rm (Shapley \cite{shapley}.)}\label{comp-label-thm}
Let $(x,y)\in X\times Y$. Then $(x,y)$ is a Nash
\linebreak[5]
equilibrium of the bimatrix
game $(A,B)$ if and only if $(x,y)$ is completely
%\linebreak[3]
labeled.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The mixed strategy $x\in X$ has label $m + j$ for some $j\in [n]$ if and
only if the $j$-th pure strategy of player 2 is a best response to $x$. By
Proposition \ref{br-played-thm}, this is a necessary and sufficient condition
for
player 2 to play his $j$-th strategy at every equilibrium where player 1
chooses $x$. The analogous holds for the strategies $y\in Y$ and player 1
playing her $i$-th strategy in response to player 2 choosing $y$.
Therefore, at an equilibrium $(x,y)$ all labels $m + j$, with $j\in [n]$,
appear either as labels of $x$ or of $y$.
Conversely, if $(x,y)$ is not completely labeled, then some
label does not appear as a label of $x$ or $y$. This label
represents a pure strategy that is played with positive
probability but is not a best response, which contradicts
the equilibrium property.
\end{proof}

A useful graphical representation of labels on the simplices $X$ and $Y$
is done by labeling the outside of each simplex according to the player's own
pure strategies that are not played, and by subdividing its interior
into closed polyhedral sets, called {\em best response
regions}, that correspond to the other player's pure
best responses.
We give an example of this construction.

\begin{example}({\rm Savani and von Stengel \cite{uvg}}.)
\label{br-game-ex}

\begin{equation}
\label{AB}

\qquad
B = \left(\begin{matrix}0&2&4\\ 3&2&0\\

the exterior facets are labeled with the pure strategy that is played

For example, the best-response region in $Y$ with label~$1$

The representation of a game and its Nash equilibria in terms of best
response regions can be translated to an equivalent construction on
polytopes.
The first step is to notice that the best-response regions can be obtained as
projections on $X$ and $Y$ of the {\em best-response facets} of
the polyhedra
\begin{equation}
\label{br-polyhedron}
\arraycolsep.2em
\begin{array}{rcll}
\overline{P}&=&\{ (x,v)\in X\times\reals& \mid B\T x\leq\1 v \},\\
\overline{Q}&=&\{ (y,u)\in Y\times\reals& \mid A y\leq\1 u \}. %
\end{array}
\end{equation}
In $\overline{P}$, these facets are the points $(x,v)\in X\times\reals$
such that $(B\T x)_j = v$, which in turn correspond to the strategies
$x\in X$ of player 1 that give exactly payoff $v$ to player~2
when he plays strategy $j$; this payoff is the
best-response payoff by the definition of $P$.
The projection of the facet defined by $(B\T x)_j = v$ to
$X$ then has label~$j$.
Analogously, the facet of $\overline{Q}$ given by the points
$(y,u)\in Y\times\reals$ such that $(A y)_i = u$ projects to the
best-response region of $Y$ with label $i$.

Given the assumptions on non-negativity of $A$ and $B\T$, we can
change coordinates to $x_i / v$ and $y_j / u$ and replace $\overline{P}$ and
$\overline{Q}$ with the {\em best-response polytopes}
\begin{equation}
\label{br-polytopes}
\arraycolsep.2em
\begin{array}{rcll}
P&=&\{\, x\in\reals^m& \mid\,x\geq\0,\ B\T x\leq\1 \},\\
Q&=&\{\, y\in\reals^n& \mid\,A y\leq\1,~ y\geq\0  \}.
\end{array}
\end{equation}
In (\ref{br-polytopes}), both $P$ and $Q$ are defined by
$m+n$ inequalities that correspond, in the listed order, to the
pure strategies of the two players with labels in $[m+n]$.
The polytope $P$ is the intersection of the $m+n$ half-spaces
that correspond to either player 1 not playing her $i$-th pure
strategy or to a best response $j$ of player~2,
where $i\in [m]$ and $j\in [n]$.
The analogous statement holds for $Q$.
Formally, a point $x\in P$ has label $k$ if and only if either
$x_k = 0$ for $k\in [m]$ or $(B\T x)_j = 1$ for $k=m+j$ with $j\in [n]$, and
a point in $Q$ has label $k$ if and only if either
$(A y)_k=1$ for $k\in [m]$ or $y_j = 0$ for $k=m+j$ with $j\in [n]$.

Hence, a point $(x,y)\in P\times Q$ is completely labeled if and only if
it satisfies the {\em complementarity condition} that states
% the quantifiers should be outside to avoid confusion
that for all $i\in[m]$ and all $j\in[n]$,
\begin{equation}
%\label{}
\begin{array}{ccrll}
x_i = 0 & \text{ or }&(Ay)_i= 1~,\\
y_j = 0 & \text{ or }&(B\T x)_j=1~.
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Therefore, if $(x,y)\in P\times Q$ is completely labeled either the
corresponding point in $\overline{P}\times\overline{Q}$
is a Nash equilibrium or $(x,y)=(\0,\0)$; we refer to the latter case
as {\em artificial equilibrium}.

We now consider some special cases of games and how they are related to each
other in terms of computational complexity.
First of all, we note that any bimatrix game can be ``symmetrized''.
This result is due to Gale, Kuhn and Tucker \cite{gale-kuhn-tucker}
for zero-sum games, while its extension to non-zero-sum games is a folklore
result.

\clearpage

\begin{proposition}\label{symmetrize-c}
Let $(A,B)$ be a bimatrix game and let $(x,y)$ be one of its Nash equilibria.
Then $(z,z)$, where $z=(x\alpha,y\beta)$ for suitable
positive scalars $\alpha$ and $\beta$, is a Nash equilibrium of the symmetric game
$(C,C\T)$, where
\begin{equation}
C = \left(
    \begin{array}{cc}
    0 & A \\
    B\T & 0
    \end{array}
    \right).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

McLennan and Tourky \cite{mclennan-tourky} have proven a result in the
opposite direction of Proposition \ref{symmetrize-c}: any symmetric game
can be translated into a {\em imitiation game}, where the payoff matrix of
player 1 is the identity matrix $I$. In any Nash equilibrium of $(I,B)$,
the mixed strategy $x$
of player 1 corresponds exactly to the symmetric equilibrium $(x,x)$ in
the symmetric game defined by the payoff matrix of player 2.
Since it takes polynomial time in the size of a matrix to calculate its
transpose, an algorithm that finds a Nash equilibrium of a bimatrix game
can be used to find a symmetric Nash equilibrium of a symmetric game.

\begin{theorem}{\rm (McLennan and Tourky \cite{mclennan-tourky}.)}
\label{imitation-thm}
The pair $(x,x)$ is a symmetric Nash equilibrium of the symmetric bimatrix
game $(C,C\T)$ if and only if there is some $y$ such that $(x,y)$ is a
Nash equilibrium of the imitation game $(I,B)$ with $B=C\T$.
\end{theorem}

Notice that Theorem \ref{imitation-thm} applies to the symmetric
equilibria of the symmetric game but not to all its Nash equilibria; there
could be non-symmetric equilibria of $(C,C\T)$ that are not found
through the imitation game, as shown in the following example.

\begin{example}({\rm Savani and von Stengel \cite{uvg}}.)
As an example, consider the
\linebreak[4]
symmetric game $(C,C\T)$ with
\begin{equation}
\label{C}
C = \left(\begin{matrix}0&3&0\\ 2&2&2\\
4&0&0\end{matrix}\right),
\qquad
C\T = \left(\begin{matrix}0&2&4\\ 3&2&0\\
0&2&0\end{matrix}\right).
\end{equation}
The corresponding imitation game is $(I,C\T)=(A,B)$, seen in
Example \ref{br-game-ex}.
Figure~\ref{imit-fig} shows the labeled mixed-strategy simplices $X$ and $Y$

are different.
In addition to the symmetric equilibrium $(x,x)$ where
$x=(\frac13,\frac23,0)$, the game has two non-symmetric equilibria in $(a,b)$
and $(b,a)$ with $a=(\frac12,\frac12,0)$ and $b=(0,\frac23,\frac13)$.
The imitation game $(A,B)$, on the other hand, has only one equilibrium
$(x,y)$, corresponding to $(x,x)$, with $y=(\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{2},0)$.

The characterization of Nash equilibria as completely labeled pairs $(x,y)$
holds for arbitrary bimatrix games. From now on, we impose a
further condition: all points in $P$ have at most $m$ labels,
and all points in $Q$ have at most $n$ labels. These games are called
{\em nondegenerate}.
This condition is required for the Lemke-Howson algorithm (see below).
% This is a loss of generality
The algorithm can also be applied to degenerate games by
lexicographic perturbation (see von Stengel \cite{vs-agt}).
In an equilibrium $(x,y)$ of a nondegenerate game each label appears
exactly once. This also means that the number of pure best response
strategies against a mixed strategy is never larger than the size of
the support of that mixed strategy.
Geometrically, this means that no point of the best response polytope $P$
lies on more than $m$ facets and no point of the best response polytope $Q$
lies on more than $n$ facets, so both $P$ and $Q$ are simple.
Furthermore, a point of
$P$ has exactly $m$ labels if and only if it is a vertex, and a point
of $Q$ has exactly $n$ labels if and only if it is a vertex. Therefore,
all completely labeled points $(x,y)$ are vertices of the best response
polytopes, and Nash equilibria are isolated points.

\begin{example}({\rm Savani and von Stengel \cite{uvg}}.)
An example of degenerate game is given by $(C,C\T)$ with
\begin{equation}
\label{Cdeg}
C = \left(\begin{matrix}0&4&0\\ 2&2&2\\
4&0&0\end{matrix}\right),
\qquad
C\T = \left(\begin{matrix}0&2&4\\ 4&2&0\\
0&2&0\end{matrix}\right).
\end{equation}
As shown in Figure \ref{deg-fig}, the mixed strategy
$x=(\frac12,\frac12,0)$, that also defines the
unique symmetric equilibrium $(x,x)$ of the game, has three pure best
responses. The Nash equilibria $(x,y)$ of the imitation game
$(I,C\T)$ are not unique, since any convex
combination of $(\frac12,\frac12,0)$ and $(\frac13,\frac13,\frac13)$
can be chosen for~$y$.

A generalization of imitation games is the class of
{\em unit vector games}, introduced by Balthasar \cite{balthasar}.
These are defined as bimatrix games of the form $(U,B)$ where the
columns of the matrix $U$ are unit vectors.
By the results above, finding a Nash equilibrium of a bimatrix game is
at least as hard as finding a Nash equilibrium of a unit vector game.
Savani and von Stengel \cite{uvg} have shown that in unit vector games
the problem of finding a completely labeled vertex of the product of the
best response polytopes $P\times Q$ can be simplified to the
problem of finding a completely labeled vertex of a single
polytope $P^l$ with a function $l$ that labels the last $n$
facets of $P$ with elements in $[m]$ (instead of elements
$m+j$ for $j\in[n]$) that encodes all information about the
game.

\begin{theorem}{\rm (Savani and von Stengel \cite{uvg}.)}
\label{unit-vector-thm}
Let $l:[n]\to [m]$ be a function, and let $(U,B)$ be the unit vector game with
$U=(e_{l(1)}\ \cdots\ e_{l(n)})$. Let
% \linebreak[6]
$N_i = \{ j\in [n]\ \mid\ l(j)=i \}$ for
$i\in [m]$, and consider the polytopes $P^l$ and $Q^l$
\begin{equation}\label{p-l}
\arraycolsep.2em
\begin{array}{rcll}
P^l&=&\{ x\in\reals^m& \mid\,x\geq\0,\ B\T x\leq\1 \}, \\
Q^l&=&\{ y\in\reals^n& \mid\,y\geq\0,\
\sum_{j\in N_i } y_j\leq 1 \hbox{ for } i\in [m] \}.
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Let $l_f$ be the labeling of the facets of $P^l$ with labels
in $[m]$ defined as follows:
\begin{equation}
\label{facet-labeling-unitv}
\begin{array}{rll}
x_i\geq 0 & \text{ has label }i & \text{ for }i\in [m], \\
(B\T x)_j \leq 1 & \text{ has label }l(j) & \text{ for }j\in [n].
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Then $x\in P^l$ is a completely labeled vertex of
$P^l\setminus\{\0\}$ (that is, has all labels in $[m]$)
if and only if there is some $y\in Q^l$ such that, after scaling,
the pair $(x,y)$ is a Nash equilibrium
of $(U,B)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $P$ and $Q$ be the best response polytopes of $(U,B)$ as
in (\ref{br-polytopes}),
and let $(x,y)\in P\times Q\setminus\{ (\0,\0) \}$ be a Nash equilibrium of
$(U,B)$. Then $(x,y)$ is completely labeled with labels in $[m + n]$. If
$x_i=0$, then $x$ has label $i\in m$.
If $x_i > 0$, then $y$ has label $i$, so $(Uy)_i = 1$.
Therefore for some $j\in [n]$ we have $y_j > 0$ and $U_j = e_i$; that is,
we have $y_j > 0$ and $l(j)=i$ for some $j\in [n]$.
Since $y_j > 0$, $x\in P$ has label $m+j$; then, $(B\T x)_j = 1$;
therefore $x\in P^l$ has label $l(j) = i$.
Hence, $x$ is a completely labeled vertex of $P^l$.

Conversely, let $x\in P^l\setminus \{ \0 \}$ be completely labeled.
If $x_i > 0$, then there is $j\in [n]$ such that $(B\T x) = j$ and
$l(j) = i$; that is, $j\in N_i$. For all $i\in [m]$ such that $x_i > 0$,
define $y$ as follows: $y_j = 1$, and
$y_h = 0$ for all $h\in N_i\setminus \{ j \}$.
Then $(x,y)\in P\times Q$ is completely labeled.
\end{proof}

We now move on the dual version of Theorem \ref{unit-vector-thm},
as given by
\linebreak[5]
Balthasar \cite{balthasar}.
We can translate the polytope $P^l$ of (\ref{p-l}) to
$P = \{ x - \1\ |\ x\in P^l \}$,
possibly multiplying all payoffs in $B$ by a
constant so that $\0$ is in the interior of $P$, which holds
if $\1$ in the interior of $P^l$, that is, all columns $b_j$
of the matrix $B$ fulfill $\1\T b_j<1$, for $j\in[n]$. Then
\begin{align*}
P   & = \{ x + \1\geq\0,\ (x + \1)\T B\leq\1 \} = \\
    & = \{ x\in\reals^m\ |\ -x_i\leq 1\text{ for }i\in [m],\
    x\T (b_j/(1 - \1\T b_j)) \leq 1\text{ for }j\in [n] \}.
\end{align*}
The polar of $P$ is then
% DON'T USE Q, especially since you come back to the old Q
% later!
\begin{equation}\label{p-l-dual}
P^\Delta = \conv(\{-e_i\ |\ i\in [m] \}\ \cup\ \{ c_j\ |\ j\in [n] \})
\end{equation}
where $c_j=b_j/(1 - \1\T b_j)$.
Since $P$ and $P^{\Delta}$ have $\0$ in their interior,
$P^{\Delta\Delta}=P$. Furthermore,
$P^\Delta $ is simplicial and its facets correspond to the vertices of $P$
and vice versa.
We label the vertices
of $P^\Delta $ as the corresponding facets in $P^l$, so the completely
labeled facets of $P^\Delta $ correspond to the completely
labeled vertices of $P^l$.
In particular, the facet corresponding to $\0$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{f-0}
\arraycolsep.3em
\begin{array}{rcl}
F_0 & = & \{ x\in P^\Delta\ \mid\ -\1\T x = 1 \}\\
    & = & \conv\{-e_i \mid i\in [m] \}.
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Theorem \ref{unit-vector-thm} then translates to the following.

\begin{theorem}{\rm (Balthasar \cite{balthasar}.)}
\label{unit-vector-dual-thm}
Let $P^\Delta $ be a labeled $m$-dimensional simplicial polytope with \0 in
its interior and vertices $e_1,\ldots,e_m,c_1,\ldots,c_n$ such that
$F_0$ in $(\ref{f-0})$ is a facet of $P^\Delta $.
Let $(U,B)$ be a unit vector game, with $U=(e_{l(1)}\ \cdots\ e_{l(n)})$
for a labeling $l:[n]\to [m]$ and $B = [\,b_1\,\cdots\,b_n\,]$, where
$b_j = c_j/(1 + \1\T c_j)$ for $j\in [n]$. Let $l_v$ be the labeling
of the vertices of $P^\Delta $ given by
\begin{equation}
\label{vert-labeling-unitv}
\arraycolsep.2em
\begin{array}{rcll}
l_v(-e_i)&=&i\quad & \text{ for }i\in [m],\\
l_v(c_j)&=&l(j)\quad & \text{ for }j\in [n].
\end{array}
\end{equation}
Then a facet $F\neq F_0$ of $P^\Delta $ with normal vector $v$ is completely
labeled if and only if $(x,y)$ is a Nash equilibrium of $(U,B)$, where
$x = (v + \1) / (\1\T (v + \1))$, so that
$x_i = 0$ if and only if $âˆ’e_i\in F$ for $i\in [m]$
and the mixed strategy $y$ is the uniform distribution on the set of
the pure best replies to $x$, which in turn correspond to all $j\in [n]$
such that $c_j$ is a vertex of $F$.
\end{theorem}

Theorem \ref{unit-vector-thm} gives a correspondence between
completely labeled vertices of $P^l$ and equilibria of the unit vector game
$(U,B)$ with the ``artificial'' equilibrium corresponding to the vertex $\0$.
Theorem \ref{unit-vector-dual-thm} gives a correspondence between
completely labeled facets of $P^\Delta $
and equilibria of $(U,B)$ with the ``artificial'' equilibrium
corresponding to the facet $F_0$ in (\ref{f-0}).

Given a bimatrix game $(A,B)$, it takes polynomial time
to write and solve the linear equations defining its best response polyhedra
$\overline{P},\overline{Q}$ and its best response polytopes $P,Q$.
It also takes polynomial time to label $\overline{P},\overline{Q}$ and $P,Q$.
Analogously, given a unit vector game $(U,B)$, it takes polynomial time
to construct and label the polytope $P^l$ and its polar.
Therefore, Theorem \ref{unit-vector-thm} gives a polynomial time
reduction from the problem 2-{\sc Nash} to the
problem {\sc Another Completely Labeled Vertex} of Table \ref{aclv} and
Theorem \ref{unit-vector-dual-thm} gives a dual reduction to the problem
{\sc Another Completely Labeled Facet} of Table \ref{aclf}.

\begin{problem}
{Another Completely Labeled Vertex}
{An $m$-dimensional simple polytope $P$ with $m+n$ facets;
a labeling $l_f:[m+n]\to [n]$;
a vertex $v_0$ of $P$ that is completely labeled by~$l_f$.}
{A vertex $v\neq v_0$ of $P$ that is completely labeled by $l_s$.}
\label{aclv}
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}
{Another Completely Labeled Facet}
{A simplicial $m$-dimensional polytope $P^\Delta$ with $m+n$ vertices;
a labeling $l_v:[m+n]\to [n]$;
a facet $F_0$ of $P^\Delta$ that is completely labeled by $l_v$.}
{A facet $F\neq F_0$ of $P^\Delta$ that is completely labeled by $l_v$.}
\label{aclf}
\end{problem}


\begin{proposition}
\label{nash-to-acl}
{\sc 2-Nash} reduces in polynomial time to
{\sc Another}
\linebreak[5]
{\sc Completely Labeled Vertex} and
{\sc Another Completely Labeled}
\linebreak[5]
{\sc Facet}.
\end{proposition}
